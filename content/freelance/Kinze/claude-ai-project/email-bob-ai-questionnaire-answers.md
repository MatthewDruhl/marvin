# Bob Derksen - AI Questionnaire Answers

Forwarded by Ryan Veatch on 2026-06-05. Original from Robert Derksen (Robert.Derksen@kinze.com) dated 2026-04-14.

---

**B-1:** We have not come across requests that are not feasible within the 14-100 wide and 11-30 tall. Maybe if we have 1 that is much taller than the width.

**B-2:** I typically round to the nearest 6" increment for developing cost calculations.

**B-3:** We generally do cost roll ups on an annual basis - start of the fiscal year in June. Although we do need to track cost changes on the purchased items such as hydraulic pumps (my opinion).

**B-4:** It is based on door size, as I typically start with the small-single and move up to the "Truss" for the largest doors. I'll do some trial and error to see where the breaks are between the column frames.

**B-5:** I typically start with "no truss", then L truss, then X-Brace, then V-truss until the calculation passes the stress parameters. We have been keeping the cylinders at 4" diam for now. Hope to get to smaller cylinders for the smaller doors in the future to save cost.

**B-6:** 115 mph wind load is standard for most of the country. Florida and gulf states will normally require 150 mph hurricane rating. We typically verify wind load with customers in the hurricane region if it is not specified in the quote request.

**B-7:** Door opening time calculations are generally dependent on column frame design and overall door weight. Typically, small single is 50 sec, Single is 64 sec, Big single is 84 sec, etc.

**B-8:** Yes, there is a naming convention. I'll work up a separate chart for the door coding - it's a bit complicated.

**B-9:** We probably have 60-70 GA drawings in the library, loosely organized by GA number.

**B-10:** Most customer questions revolve around the information shown on the side view of the drawing. For example, the most frequent question is about the clearance needed inside the building for the top of the door travel upwards and inwards. Second are questions about the clearance height below the door when it is open.

**B-11:** To begin with, I would suggest a manual review of the parametrically generated drawings, then can be automated once a particular size has been reviewed and approved.

**B-12:** Factory orders are unique for each end product and the standard 4th shift (MRP) system is used to release orders to the factory. We do create a full BOM, and drawings are generated for the individual piece parts and weldments. We are working on 3D modeling of the doors to help speed up this process.

**B-13:** The MRP system releases a "packet" for each individual part number to be manufactured. Piece parts to the appropriate fabrication department and weldments to the welding department. When a piece part order is completed it is logged accordingly into MRP, and the parts are moved to an inventory location which is also noted in MRP.

**B-14:** Currently, shipping manually prepares the door for shipment by queueing up the parts as they are completed and deciding on how to best configure the shipment. This is an area of great opportunity for improvement!

**B-15:** Total steel weight can be calculated from the CAD model, but items like hardware, welding material, etc are not included in the calculation.

**B-16:** Typically the engineering calculations are sufficient so far. We are working on adding flexibility to the calculations.

**B-17:** We do see occasional failures... Then use manual intervention and will need to rely on additional engineering review by a licensed Professional Structural Engineer.

**B-18:** I think it would be good to have the AI flag areas of concern by communicating the calculated safety factor, as that is what typically drives the pass/fail.
